I confess... I take too many pictures of my food

I need to be open about something: I've spent way too much time in my kitchen. But this is not a regret, as I've learned to be a pretty great hack chef.

I started learning fairly young...

The first thing I learned to make was pie. I must have been 12 years old. It was cherry pie to be exact. My grandfather taught me how to make the dough from scratch, and how to amp up the flavor of canned cherry filling with fruit liqueur. I recall being hooked. From there I was basically on my own journey in the kitchen. And even though my family didn't have significant means when I was young, I had a lot of room to make whatever I could with the ingredients we did have on hand. I don't know how many times I made chocolate peanut butter oatmeal drop cookies back then, but I can tell you that my friends loved them, and the recipe page has seen better days.

Not a Chef

Unlike our recently fallen antihero, Anthony Bourdain, I am not a trained chef, nor have I ever wanted to be one. The thought of spending hours in a hot kitchen cooking for over a hundred heads a night has absolutely zero appeal. I cook for enjoyment. It's meditation. And I throw the occasional dinner party (though less so now that I'm in Launchcode!).

Some Hits

What follows is a brief collection of where my culinary skills have taken me. I like making everything.

First Course: Bread

We'll start with the simplest of breads... the lowly, non-yeasted biscuit. Oh how I love thee. Especially with hot, spicy honey. Good biscuits are surprisingly hard to find at restaurants. But they really are simple to make. Mix a ton of butter with flour, baking powder, and salt until you have a rough mixture of pea-sized flakes, incorporate some buttermilk, mix gently by hand until just combined, then quickly cut out the shapes. Once you get it down, you'll never order another biscuit brick again. I make squares instead of the traditional shape. I don't want odds and ends of dough. I want it all.



Admittedly, this is another pretty easy one: foccacia. Though yeasted, it's nearly foolproof to make. It has a really nice crunchy, crispy exterior, and soft interior. Good for basic sandwiches, but I think it's best just on it's own. No dipping oil or butter required. Second Course: More Carbs

Anyone here not like pizza? I like it all the ways. But I have a particular affection for the STL style crust. Not so much the provel, but I'll still eat it. I enjoy making the STL crust because it's:
  1. Suuuuuper easy to make. Like 5 minutes (plus time to rest).
  2. You can load it up with toppings, as nature intended.
  3. I can eat an entire pizza and not feel guilty.
I don't think I need any more reasons.



While we are on Italian-inspired foods, how about some gnocchi? You really must try making it once. It's work, yes. But with the right tools it's not too bad. Just bake the potato, don't boil it (boiling it introduces too much water into the spud). For tools, it's best to have a ricer and a gnocchi rolling block. That's pretty much it!



Linguine and clams. This is probably my favorite pasta dish to eat (other than pesto). Canned clams are ok. But it's better with fresh ones.


Third Course: Proteins

We will transition from the prior section with the combo of carb and protein: maki sushi. Alton Brown showed me how to roll years ago. It's not something I do very often anymore, because it is a considerable amount of prep. But an impressive dish to make for others.



I also like smoking fish. I use a device called a Big Chief and it makes perfect smoked salmon. That's cherry and alder smoked in the photo.



I tend to eat a little less meat these days, but I still jam on a smashburger from time to time. And when I do, I grind my own (I use a food processor, but basically the same thing, less difficult to clean). This burger was made with a combo of hangar steak and short ribs. It did not suck.
